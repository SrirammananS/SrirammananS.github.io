Basic Concepts & Definitions
Q. Define Crime.
Crime is an act or omission that violates established laws and is punishable by the state. It represents socially harmful behavior that threatens public order, safety, and welfare. Crimes are classified into various categories including felonies, misdemeanors, and infractions, each carrying different levels of punishment from fines to imprisonment.
Q. Define Tort.
A tort is a civil wrong that causes harm or injury to another person, for which the injured party can seek compensation. Unlike crimes, torts are private matters between individuals. Common examples include negligence, defamation, trespass, and assault. The remedy is typically monetary damages rather than criminal punishment.
Q. Define Misdemeanour.
A misdemeanour is a minor criminal offense that is less serious than a felony but more serious than an infraction. These crimes typically carry penalties of less than one year in jail, fines, or community service. Examples include petty theft, simple assault, public intoxication, and minor traffic violations.
Q. Define Law.
Law is a system of rules and regulations established by governmental authority to maintain social order, protect rights, and ensure justice. It encompasses statutory laws, common law, and constitutional provisions. Laws provide framework for acceptable behavior, prescribe punishments for violations, and establish procedures for legal proceedings and dispute resolution.
Cyber Crime Fundamentals
Q. Define Cyber Crime.
Cyber crime refers to criminal activities carried out using computers, networks, or digital devices. These include hacking, identity theft, online fraud, cyberbullying, and data breaches. Cyber crimes transcend geographical boundaries, making investigation and prosecution challenging. They target individuals, organizations, and governments, causing financial, emotional, and reputational damage.
Q. Define Cyber Criminology.
Cyber criminology is the scientific study of cyber crimes, their causes, patterns, and prevention methods. It combines traditional criminological theories with digital forensics and technology understanding. This field examines criminal behavior in cyberspace, develops profiling techniques for cyber criminals, and formulates strategies for crime prevention and investigation.
Q. Define Cyber Space.
Cyber space is the virtual environment created by interconnected computer networks, including the internet. It's a digital realm where information, communication, and transactions occur. Cyber space has no physical boundaries, operates 24/7 globally, and encompasses websites, social media, online databases, and digital communication platforms.
Q. Define Cyber Criminals.
Cyber criminals are individuals or groups who commit crimes using digital technology and computer networks. They include hackers, fraudsters, identity thieves, and online predators. These criminals exploit technological vulnerabilities, use sophisticated tools, and often operate across international borders, making detection and prosecution challenging for law enforcement agencies.
Q. Define Cyber Appellate Court.
Cyber Appellate Court is a specialized judicial body established under the Information Technology Act to hear appeals against orders of adjudicating officers. It deals with cyber crime cases, data protection violations, and IT-related disputes. The court has expertise in technology matters and provides faster resolution of cyber-related legal issues.
Contemporary Crime Forms
Q. Enumerate Contemporary forms of cybercrimes against property (at least 4).
Contemporary cybercrimes against property include: 1) Online banking fraud and credit card theft, 2) Cryptocurrency theft and wallet hacking, 3) Intellectual property piracy and software theft, 4) E-commerce fraud and fake online stores, 5) Ransomware attacks encrypting data for money, and 6) Digital asset manipulation and unauthorized transfers.
Q. Explain one Contemporary form of crime.
Ransomware attacks represent a contemporary cybercrime where malicious software encrypts victim's data, demanding payment for decryption keys. These attacks target hospitals, schools, businesses, and government agencies, causing operational paralysis. Criminals use sophisticated encryption, cryptocurrency payments, and global networks, making investigation complex and recovery difficult.
Q. Define Forms of Organised Crimes.
Organised crimes are systematic criminal activities conducted by structured groups for profit. These include drug trafficking, human trafficking, money laundering, arms smuggling, and cybercrime syndicates. Organised crimes involve hierarchical structures, division of labor, corruption of officials, and use of violence or intimidation to maintain control and silence witnesses.
Q. List types of Cyber Crimes (at least 4).
Major types of cyber crimes include: 1) Hacking and unauthorized access, 2) Identity theft and impersonation, 3) Online fraud and phishing, 4) Cyberbullying and harassment, 5) Data theft and privacy breaches, 6) Malware distribution, 7) Cyber terrorism, and 8) Online child exploitation and abuse.
Q. Define Cyber Crimes against Property.
Cyber crimes against property involve unauthorized access, theft, or damage to digital assets and information systems. These include online banking fraud, credit card theft, software piracy, cryptocurrency theft, and data manipulation. Such crimes cause significant financial losses and compromise the integrity of digital property and electronic transactions.
White Collar & Economic Crimes
Q. Expand and explain WCC.
WCC stands for White Collar Crime. These are non-violent crimes committed by business professionals for financial gain. Coined by Edwin Sutherland, WCC includes embezzlement, fraud, bribery, insider trading, and tax evasion. These crimes are characterized by deceit, concealment, and violation of trust, often causing massive economic damage.
Q. Define White Collar Crime.
White Collar Crime refers to financially motivated, non-violent crimes committed by business and government professionals. These include corporate fraud, embezzlement, money laundering, and tax evasion. Such crimes exploit positions of trust and authority, often involving complex schemes that cause significant economic harm to individuals, organizations, and society.
Q. Expand and explain GRC.
GRC stands for Governance, Risk, and Compliance. It's an integrated approach helping organizations align IT with business objectives while managing risk and meeting compliance requirements. GRC involves establishing policies, identifying risks, implementing controls, and ensuring regulatory compliance to protect organizational assets and reputation.
Q. Define Tax Evasion.
Tax evasion is the illegal practice of not paying taxes owed by underreporting income, inflating deductions, or hiding money. It involves deliberate falsification of tax returns or failure to file returns entirely. Tax evasion is a criminal offense punishable by fines, penalties, and imprisonment, distinct from legal tax avoidance strategies.
Q. Define Money Laundering.
Money laundering is the process of making illegally obtained money appear legitimate by passing it through complex financial transactions and legitimate businesses. It involves three stages: placement, layering, and integration. Money laundering facilitates criminal activities by obscuring the source of illegal funds and is a serious economic crime globally.
Q. Define Fraudulent Cheque.
A fraudulent cheque is a forged, altered, or counterfeit check used to illegally obtain money or goods. This includes fake signatures, altered amounts, stolen cheque books, or bounced cheques issued with criminal intent. Fraudulent cheques cause financial losses to banks, businesses, and individuals, constituting a serious economic offense.
Criminal Techniques & Methods
Q. Define Modus Operandi.
Modus Operandi (M.O.) refers to the habitual method or pattern used by criminals to commit crimes. It includes specific techniques, tools, timing, and behavioral patterns that criminals repeatedly use. Understanding M.O. helps law enforcement identify suspects, link crimes, predict future offenses, and develop investigation strategies based on criminal behavior patterns.
Q. Argue: Modus Operandi is habitual.
Modus Operandi is indeed habitual because criminals tend to repeat successful methods that minimize risk and maximize gain. Psychological comfort with familiar techniques, learned skills, and successful outcomes reinforce repetitive behavior. This habitual nature allows investigators to identify patterns, link crimes, and predict criminal behavior, making M.O. a crucial investigative tool.
Q. Define DDOS attack.
DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) attack involves overwhelming a target system with traffic from multiple compromised devices to make services unavailable to legitimate users. Attackers use botnets to flood servers, websites, or networks with requests, causing system crashes or slowdowns. DDoS attacks disrupt business operations and are commonly used for extortion or activism.
Q. Define MIM attack.
MIM (Man-in-the-Middle) attack occurs when an attacker secretly intercepts and relays communications between two parties who believe they're communicating directly. The attacker can eavesdrop, modify messages, or steal sensitive information like passwords and credit card details. MIM attacks exploit unsecured networks and weak encryption protocols.
Q. Define Hackers.
Hackers are individuals with advanced computer skills who gain unauthorized access to systems or networks. They're categorized as white hat (ethical), black hat (malicious), or gray hat (mixed motives). Malicious hackers steal data, disrupt services, or commit fraud, while ethical hackers help identify security vulnerabilities to improve system protection.
Q. Define Tools adopted by Cyber Criminals.
Cyber criminals use various sophisticated tools including malware, phishing kits, keyloggers, and botnets. Other tools include password crackers, network scanners, social engineering techniques, and encryption software. These tools help criminals gain unauthorized access, steal information, hide their identity, and execute attacks while avoiding detection by security systems.
Q. Define Penetration of Networks.
Network penetration involves gaining unauthorized access to computer networks by exploiting security vulnerabilities. Attackers use techniques like port scanning, vulnerability assessment, and social engineering to breach network defenses. Successful penetration allows criminals to steal data, install malware, or gain control of network resources for malicious purposes.
Criminological Theories & Theorists
Q. Enumerate 4 theories of criminology related to Cybercrime.
Four key criminological theories for cybercrime are: 1) Routine Activities Theory - explaining cyber victimization through online activities, 2) Social Learning Theory - criminal behavior learned through digital interactions, 3) Differential Association Theory - criminal values transmitted online, and 4) Rational Choice Theory - cost-benefit analysis in cybercrime decisions.
Q. Explain Sutherland's contribution to Criminology.
Edwin Sutherland revolutionized criminology by introducing the concept of White Collar Crime and developing Differential Association Theory. He argued that criminal behavior is learned through social interaction, not inherited. His work shifted focus from individual pathology to social processes, emphasizing that crime occurs across all social classes, not just among the poor.
Q. Define Sutherland.
Edwin Sutherland (1883-1950) was an American sociologist and criminologist known as the "father of white-collar crime". He developed Differential Association Theory, coined the term "white-collar crime," and emphasized that criminal behavior is learned through social interaction. His work fundamentally changed how society understands crime and criminal behavior.
Q. Define Marcus Felson.
Marcus Felson is a contemporary criminologist who co-developed Routine Activities Theory with Lawrence Cohen. This theory explains crime occurrence through the convergence of motivated offenders, suitable targets, and absence of capable guardians. Felson's work significantly influences crime prevention strategies and environmental criminology approaches.
Q. Define Richard Cloward.
Richard Cloward was an American sociologist who, with Lloyd Ohlin, developed Differential Opportunity Theory. This theory explains how individuals pursue illegitimate means when legitimate opportunities are blocked. Cloward's work bridges strain theory and social learning theory, emphasizing the role of opportunity structures in criminal behavior.
Q. Define Cloward and Ohlin.
Cloward and Ohlin developed Differential Opportunity Theory, which explains delinquency through blocked legitimate opportunities and available illegitimate ones. They identified three types of delinquent subcultures: criminal (focused on theft), conflict (gang violence), and retreatist (drug use). Their theory combines strain theory with opportunity structure analysis.
Q. What is the main criticism of Differential Opportunity Theory?
The main criticism is that it oversimplifies the relationship between opportunity and crime. Critics argue it doesn't adequately explain why some individuals in similar circumstances don't turn to crime, ignores individual agency and choice, and assumes clear distinctions between legitimate and illegitimate opportunities that may not exist in reality.
Q. Define Suitable Target.
In Routine Activities Theory, a suitable target refers to persons or property that are attractive to potential offenders. Suitability depends on target value, visibility, accessibility, and portability (VIVA). In cyberspace, suitable targets include unprotected systems, valuable data, or vulnerable individuals with poor digital security practices.
Q. Define Capable Guardian.
A capable guardian in Routine Activities Theory refers to people or security measures that can prevent crime through their presence or surveillance. This includes police, security guards, neighbors, alarm systems, or surveillance cameras. In cyberspace, capable guardians include firewalls, antivirus software, system administrators, and cybersecurity measures.
Media & Behavioral Aspects
Q. Will Media influence Human Behaviour?
Yes, media significantly influences human behavior through repeated exposure, modeling, and social learning. Media shapes attitudes, values, and behavioral patterns by presenting role models, normalizing certain behaviors, and creating social expectations. Both positive and negative behaviors can be learned from media consumption, particularly affecting children and impressionable individuals.
Q. Can Media influence Criminal behaviour?
Media can influence criminal behavior through glorification of violence, detailed crime portrayals, and copycat effects. Exposure to violent content may desensitize individuals, provide criminal techniques, or inspire imitation. However, the relationship is complex, with media being one of many factors contributing to criminal behavior alongside personal, social, and environmental influences.
Q. Relate Media and its impact on Crime.
Media impacts crime through social learning, agenda setting, and moral panic creation. It can inspire copycat crimes, spread criminal techniques, or create fear disproportionate to actual crime rates. Conversely, media also aids crime prevention through awareness campaigns, reporting criminal activities, and supporting law enforcement efforts through public cooperation.
Q. Define Social Media.
Social media refers to digital platforms enabling users to create, share content, and interact with others online. These include Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, and WhatsApp. Social media facilitates communication, networking, and information sharing but also creates new venues for cyberbullying, fraud, privacy violations, and criminal activities.
Q. Define Media.
Media encompasses all channels of mass communication including television, radio, newspapers, internet, and social platforms. It serves to inform, educate, entertain, and influence public opinion. Media plays crucial roles in crime reporting, shaping public perception of safety, and facilitating both crime prevention and sometimes criminal behavior through various content.
Q. Define Psychology.
Psychology is the scientific study of human behavior, mental processes, and emotions. In criminology, psychology helps understand criminal motivation, personality disorders, cognitive distortions, and behavioral patterns. It contributes to criminal profiling, treatment programs, risk assessment, and developing strategies for crime prevention and offender rehabilitation.
Legal & Procedural Terms
Q. Define FIR.
FIR (First Information Report) is the first formal complaint filed with police about a cognizable offense. It initiates criminal proceedings, contains basic details of the crime, and is prepared based on oral or written complaint. FIR is a crucial legal document that sets the criminal justice process in motion and cannot be altered without court permission.
Q. Expand FIR. How many copies are made?
FIR stands for First Information Report. Typically, three copies of FIR are made: one remains with the police station, one is sent to the concerned magistrate, and one copy is given to the informant free of cost. Some jurisdictions may require additional copies for different authorities or record-keeping purposes.
Q. Define Charge Sheet.
A charge sheet is a formal document filed by police in court after completing investigation, containing charges against the accused with supporting evidence. It includes details of the offense, evidence collected, witness statements, and legal provisions applicable. The charge sheet formally accuses individuals and initiates the trial process in criminal cases.
Q. Define Trial.
A trial is the formal judicial proceeding where evidence is presented before a court to determine guilt or innocence of the accused. It involves examination of witnesses, presentation of evidence, legal arguments, and judicial decision. Trials ensure due process, fair hearing, and justice through systematic evaluation of facts and law.
Q. Define Arrest.
Arrest is the legal act of taking a person into custody for alleged criminal activity. It involves physical restraint or formal declaration limiting the person's freedom of movement. Arrest requires reasonable suspicion or warrant, and the arrested person must be informed of charges and rights, including the right to legal representation.
Q. Define Seizure.
Seizure refers to the legal act of taking possession of property, documents, or evidence by law enforcement authorities during investigation. It involves taking control of items that may be relevant to criminal proceedings. Seizure must follow legal procedures, be properly documented, and the seized items must be preserved for court proceedings.
Q. Define Court.
A court is a judicial institution with authority to interpret and apply law, adjudicate disputes, and administer justice. Courts have jurisdiction to hear cases, examine evidence, and deliver judgments. The court system includes various levels from trial courts to appellate courts, each with specific powers and responsibilities in the justice system.
Q. Define Bail.
Bail is the temporary release of an accused person from custody pending trial, usually secured by monetary guarantee or surety bond. It ensures the accused appears for trial while allowing them to remain free. Bail can be granted or denied based on factors like offense severity, flight risk, and public safety concerns.
Q. Define Cognizable Offence.
A cognizable offense is a serious crime where police can arrest without warrant and start investigation without court permission. These include murder, rape, theft, and assault. Police can register FIR immediately and begin investigation. Cognizable offenses are generally non-bailable and carry imprisonment of three years or more.
Q. Define Plea of Guilty.
A plea of guilty is the accused person's formal admission in court of having committed the charged offense. This plea waives the right to trial and moves directly to sentencing. The court must ensure the plea is voluntary, informed, and supported by factual basis before accepting it and determining appropriate punishment.
Crime Prevention & Control
Q. Define Role of NGO's in Prevention of Crime.
NGOs play crucial roles in crime prevention through community awareness, victim support, rehabilitation programs, and advocacy. They conduct educational campaigns, provide legal aid, run counseling services, and work with marginalized communities. NGOs bridge gaps between government agencies and citizens, promoting social justice and crime prevention through grassroots initiatives.
Q. Define Role of Victim of Cybercrimes in CJA.
Victims of cybercrimes play vital roles in Criminal Justice Administration by reporting crimes, providing evidence, testifying in court, and cooperating with investigations. They help identify perpetrators, explain technical aspects, and demonstrate crime impact. Victim participation is essential for successful prosecution and helps improve cybercrime prevention strategies.
Q. Define Social Control.
Social control refers to mechanisms used by society to regulate individual behavior and maintain order. It includes formal controls (laws, police, courts) and informal controls (family, peers, social norms). Social control prevents deviance, promotes conformity, and ensures social stability through various institutions and socialization processes.
Q. Enlist causes of Cyber Crime (at least 4).
Major causes of cybercrime include: 1) Rapid technological advancement outpacing security measures, 2) Anonymity and low risk of detection in cyberspace, 3) High financial rewards with minimal physical risk, 4) Lack of cybersecurity awareness among users, 5) Weak law enforcement capabilities, and 6) Easy access to hacking tools and techniques online.
Law Enforcement
Q. Define Police.
Police are law enforcement officers responsible for maintaining public order, preventing crime, and enforcing laws. Their duties include patrolling, investigating crimes, arresting offenders, and ensuring public safety. Police serve as the first responders to criminal incidents and play crucial roles in the criminal justice system through prevention, detection, and investigation.
Q. Define Suspect.
A suspect is a person believed to have committed or been involved in a crime based on evidence, witness testimony, or circumstantial factors. Suspects have legal rights including presumption of innocence, right to remain silent, and right to legal representation. Being a suspect doesn't imply guilt until proven in court.
Q. Define Law Enforcement.
Law enforcement refers to activities undertaken by government agencies to ensure compliance with laws and maintain public safety. It involves prevention, detection, investigation, and prosecution of crimes. Law enforcement agencies include police, specialized units, and regulatory bodies working together to uphold rule of law and protect citizens.
Q. Define Criminal Profiling.
Criminal profiling is the process of analyzing crime scene evidence and behavioral patterns to create psychological and demographic profiles of unknown offenders. It helps investigators narrow suspect pools, predict behavior, and develop investigation strategies. Profiling combines psychology, criminology, and forensic science to understand criminal behavior patterns.
Q. Define Incident Response.
Incident response is the systematic approach to addressing and managing security breaches or cyberattacks. It involves preparation, detection, containment, eradication, recovery, and lessons learned. Effective incident response minimizes damage, reduces recovery time, and helps prevent similar incidents through improved security measures and procedures.
Organized Crime
Q. Define Organized Crime.
Organized crime refers to criminal activities conducted by structured groups operating for profit over extended periods. These groups have hierarchical structures, division of labor, corruption networks, and use violence or intimidation. Examples include drug cartels, human trafficking rings, and cybercrime syndicates that operate across jurisdictions.
Q. Define Terrorism.
Terrorism involves the use of violence or threat of violence against civilians to achieve political, religious, or ideological objectives. Terrorists seek to create fear, gain publicity, and influence government policies through attacks on symbolic targets. Modern terrorism includes cyberterrorism targeting critical infrastructure and information systems.
Theoretical Understanding
Q. Can theories help in understanding the real-time space?
Yes, theories provide frameworks for understanding, predicting, and explaining phenomena in real-time situations. Criminological theories help analyze criminal behavior patterns, predict crime trends, and develop prevention strategies. They bridge abstract concepts with practical applications, enabling better decision-making and policy formulation in dynamic, real-world contexts.
Q. Theories can influence the understanding of the reader - Opine.
Theories significantly influence reader understanding by providing conceptual lenses, analytical frameworks, and interpretive models. They shape how information is processed, what aspects are emphasized, and how conclusions are drawn. Different theoretical perspectives can lead to varying interpretations of the same phenomena, highlighting the importance of considering multiple theoretical approaches.
